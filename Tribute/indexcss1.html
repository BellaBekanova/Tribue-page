Elmeskhan Khagundokova

Russian-Circassian noblewoman who became a heroine of France

She had many names. By birth -Elmeskhan Khagundokova, then Gali Bazhenova and, finally, Irene du Loire. Circassian Beauty, a rank-and-file of the army of fashion, the Commander of a mobile hospital, a Resistance hero, and the godmother of a regiment in the Foreign Legion.

Born on September 6th, 1898 in St Petersburg, Russia. Elmeskhan came from an aristocratic noble (warki) Kabardian family of the Khagundokovs.

Father: General Konstantin Nikolaevich Khagundokov was the commander of the second brigade of the Wild Division of the Russian Imperial Army and the owner of many awards for his courage and heroism, including the St. George Cross and St. George weapons.

Mother: Elizaveta Emilyevna Bredova came from an ancient family of German aristocrats of the German Empire from Polabian Slavs, relatives of aristocratic families.

During WWI, Elmeshan, a graduate from Smolny Institute went to Kislovodsk (a town in South Russia) with her mother and sisters, where she was admitted to the hospital as a nurse.

There she met her future husband Nikolai Bazhenov, who was brought to the hospital with a serious head injury. Young people fell in love and got married in St. Petersburg. She changed her name to Gali Bozhenova after marriage.

Soon after the revolution, their son Nikolai was born. The family was forced to flee to China, where Gali's husband Nikolai Bazhenov fell seriously ill and died.



In 1922, Gali and her son moved to France, which gave shelter to many of Russian emigrants, including her father's large family. In 1923, the "Russian beauty" Bazhenova, as the Parisian fashion magazines called her, became the darling of secular society.

Coco Chanel, on the advice of Prince Kutuzov, invites Gali to work, and her face begins to flicker on the pages of the most popular publications - Femina and Vogue. The exquisite beauty became the "secular fashion model" of the Chanel House. This was one of the categories of models of that time: fashion models with a rare appearance or a high-profile title received dresses to go out to social events.



Later, Gali opens her own Elmis Fashion House, which specialized in tailoring exquisite evening dresses with embroidery. During the years of the economic crisis, the Elmis Fashion House, like most Russian fashion houses, could not withstand competition and closed.

In 1934, Gali marries her second husband, Count Stanislas du Loire, converts to Catholicism and changes her name to Irene. Stanislav was in the civil service, a senator, the son of a marquis.

As proof of his love, Count Du Loire encouraged her thirst for entrepreneurship, a thirst for action and giving, until his death in 1980.

During the war, Countess Irene du Loire organized mobile operating rooms based on trucks, which allowed them to work literally on the front line. There were twenty such trucks. The idea turned out to be more than successful. Her mobile hospitals were nicknamed "flying", and she became famous throughout France.

French General Dubost recalled: “This organization she founded included about two hundred people, a quarter of them surgeons. The hospital in a short time was deployed near the front to operate on the most severe wounded. Mobile surgery No. 1 has always followed its patroness, full of dedication and unwavering courage.”

She then set up a mobile surgical hospital in North Africa, where she treated thousands of typhoid and cholera patients, and moved with her medical staff to Italy at the height of the war, where she saved hundreds of lives during the historic battle of Monte Cassino.

The choice of the location of the hospitals was not accidental. The entire combat path coincided with the movements of her beloved son Nikolai, who, having survived in the hellish heat of the war, died untimely in 1954 from a brain tumor.

The whole world began to provide her financial assistance. She used all the donations to expand her activities, and it turned out that by the end of the war, Irene had saved over seveal tens of thousans of lives.

She has always been where the danger is. Always ahead. After each surgical operation, supervised personally, she was with the patients until they are awake to cheer them up, like a mother. The Countess du Loire managed to get ahead of the advancing Allied troops and was the first to cross through the Franco-German border with her hospital.

She was the favorite of French Marshal Alphonse Juin, who, when he first saw the lady on the front line, told her:



-Go away, there's nothing for you to do here!
-What do you care?- Irene replied - I'm not a soldier, and you have no right to command me.
- This is my area! I'm in command here!
-Soldiers - yes! Not for me!
And she stayed. Later they became friends, and when she was awarded the Order of the Commander, the marshal himself came to present it.

On July 14, 1945, a parade took place in Paris in honor of the victory over Nazi Germany. Among those who received military honors and enthusiastic greetings of the people was Irene, who passed in a white ambulance jeep along the Champs Elysees.

She died in 1985 at the age of 87. The day before she was diagnosed with leukemia and predicted the date of death. And in this situation, she behaved with dignity: she put on a beautiful dress, a pearl necklace, made a beautiful hairstyle and waited.

Galya's funeral was in the category of a French marshal. Her coffin, on a cannon carriage, accompanied by a regiment of the Foreign Legion, was taken to the cemetery of Sainte-Genevieve-des-Bois, where her son rests. Gali was buried next to him.

Parisian magazines wrote: “All Paris said goodbye to her. France said goodbye to her. Numerous Russian emigrants who had long ago become French or who were born French said goodbye to her.” All of The First Cavalry Regiment of the Foreign Legion said goodbye to her.

Irene, tears in my eyes, We came to tell you: "Farewell..."
